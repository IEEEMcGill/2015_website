Technical Talk
Planning & Optimization of Wireless Communication Networks: Job opportunities for Women in Electrical Engineering


Who: McGill IEEE Student Branch & Women in Engineering

Speaker: Saeedeh Parsaee fard

When: 17:00 to 18:00, 25th November, 2014

Where: FDA5, McGill University

  • Abstract: The tremendous popularity of wireless technologies during the last two decades has created a considerable expansion of wireless networks in size, number of users and verity of applications. Consequently, the Telecomm Industry has become one of the most influence economic sectors of developed countries, and different wireless mobile operators have been trying to attract the mobile users with offering the high quality and seamless voice and data services. To reach this goal, the precise cellular network planning is required to provide the coverage and capacity for mobile users. However, due to the mobility of users and inherent dynamic nature of wireless channels, performance monitoring and optimization of system parameters are essential to maintain the quality of services and attract the new users. This life cycle of planning, monitoring and optimization brings the new class of high tech. job opportunities for the electrical engineers. In this talk, basic structure of cellular wireless networks is presented followed by introducing the related dynamic planning, performance monitoring and optimization cycle. Then, we will discuss about related jobs and required qualifications

  • Speaker Biography: Saeedeh Parsaeefard (Saeideh Parsaei Fard) received the B.Sc. and M.Sc. degrees from Amirkabir Universiy of Technology, Tehran, Iran, in 2003 and 2006, respectively, and the Ph.D. degree in electrical and computer engineering from Tarbiat Modares University, Tehran, in 2012. She is currently a Post-Doctoral Research Fellow in the Department of Electrical and Computer Engineering at the McGill University, Canada. From November 2010 to October 2011, she was a Visiting Ph.D. Student with the Department of Electrical Engineering, University of California, Los Angeles, CA, U.S.A. She is an IEEE member and has more than 17 journal and conference papers. She has seven-year experience in the planning and optimization of wireless networks in the largest mobile operator of Middle East. Her current research interests include the applications of robust optimization theory and game theory on the resource allocation of next generation, cognitive and virtualized wireless networks.

IEEE RAS Talk
A Half Century of Satellite Communications


Speaker: Peter Garland, Director Advanced Programs of MDA Corporation

When: 11:45 to 13:30, November 22, 2013

Where: FDA5 (3450 University, near the Engineering Student Centre)


  • Abstract: This will be a perspective on satellite communications from someone whose career started in precursor technologies to Satellites (short wave radio) and who has grown up with the evolution of satellite communications through major intercontinental trunking, to connecting smaller nations to the international telephone network and through a period when there was a real debate as to if satellite communications would disappear due to the introduction of fibre optics. The talk will look at the early days of satellite communications with Telstar and Relay, but will also look at the important role satellites have played in the connecting of Canadians over the past fifty years. Finally there will be an update of where satellites play a major role today in TV and the Broadband Internet and some projections of how satellites will play an important role in the future, especially in the world of "communications anywhere" and Satellite on the Move (SOTM).

  • Speaker Biography: Peter has worked on Satellite Communications in Canada for over 30 Years. During that time he has been involved in nearly every program where the state of art was moved forward. In the mid nineties he led studies that eventually resulted in the flight of an advanced Ka Band digital payload on the ANIK F2 satellite. Later on, he led a team that was instrumental in developing and promoting the DVB-RCS standard as the first worldwide standard for two way satellite communications. Peter's career in Communications started as an apprentice in a Short Wave Transmitting station in his native United Kingdom. As a young man he was also privileged to be part of early Satellite Communications activities at Goonhilly Radio station in the West of England. His only time away from Communications was a spell of seven years spent at the University of Birmingham (UK) designing payloads for X Ray Astronomy missions, including on one of the first Space Shuttle flights. Peter currently leads Advanced Programs within MDA Satellite Systems (Montreal).

Technical Talk
Fundamental Limits of Caching


Speaker: Dr. Mohammad Ali Maddah-Ali, Bell Labs

When: 14:00 - 15:00, April 5, 2013

Where: McConnell Engineering Building, room 603

  • Abstract: Caching is a technique to reduce peak traffic rates by prefetching popular content in memories at the end users. Conventionally, cache memories are exploited by delivering requested contents in part locally rather than through the network. In this talk, we present a novel caching approach that can achieve a significantly larger reduction in peak rate compared to previously known caching schemes. In particular, the improvement can be on the order of the number of end users in the network. In the proposed approach, the cache placement is carefully designed in order to create multicasting opportunities even among users with different demands. Using an information theoretic argument, we show that the proposed scheme is within a constant factor from "capacity" for all values of the problem parameters. This is a joint work with Urs Niesen.

  • Speaker Biography: Mohammad Ali Maddah-Ali received the PhD from the University of Waterloo, Waterloo, ON, Canada. From March 2007 to December 2007, he was working at the Wireless Technology Laboratories, Nortel Networks, Ottawa, ON, Canada, in a joint program between University of Waterloo and Nortel Networks. From January 2008 to August 2010, he was a Postdoctoral Fellow at the Wireless Foundations Center, Department of Electrical Engineering and Computer Sciences, the University of California at Berkeley. Since September 2010, he has been with Bell Laboratories, Alcatel-Lucent, Holmdel, NJ, as a communication networks research scientist. His research interests include wireless communications and network information theory.

IEEE RAS Talk
A Multi-modal approach for coral reef data collection


Speaker: Dr. Gregory Dudek, James McGill Professor, McGill University

When: 5:30PM - 6:30PM, November 19, 2012

Where: McConnell Engineering Building, room 437

  • Abstract: Our work deals with sensor-based mobile robotics. That is, the use of vision and other sensors to enable the function of autonomous robotics systems. In this talk I will address the development of underwater data-collection systems, specifically for domains such as coral reefs. This is a challenging domain since tele-operation is very difficult and so there is a premium on robotic autonomy. Our work has taken two main branches: the use of robots as an assistant for a human diver, where human-robot interaction is a key requirement, and the use of a group of heterogeneous vehicles with complementary abilities. In the former case, we have develop a gesture-based dialog system via which a robot can be controlled and even programmed by a diver working with it underwater. This approach combines tracking and dialog management, and has also led to the development of a unique class of fiducial markers. In the latter case, we are interested in using an air vehicle for long range situation awareness and the detection of possible objects of interest. These targets can then be examined in greater detail by our underwater vehicle which is directed to automatically select the most notable visual features.

  • Speaker Biography: Gregory Dudek is a Professor with the School of Computer Science and a member of the McGill Research Centre for Intelligent Machines (CIM). In 2010 he was also awarded the Canadian Image Processing and Pattern Recognition Award for Research Excellence and also for Service to the Research Community. He obtained his PhD in Computer Science (computational vision) from the University of Toronto. He has published over 200 research papers on subjects including visual object description and recognition, robotic navigation and map construction, distributed system design and biological perception. He has chaired and been otherwise involved in numerous national and international conferences and professional activities concerned with Robotics, Machine Sensing and Computer Vision.

Technical Talk
Cardiovascular Magnetic Resonance Imaging: An Emerging Field in Clinical Practice


Speaker: Dr. Mehdi Hedjazi Moghari, Research Fellow at Harvard Medical Center

When: 14:00 - 15:00, November 12, 2012

Where: McConnell Engineering Building, room 603

Pizza and pop will be served.

There will be 15 minutes of networking and socializing prior to the talk

  • Abstract: Cardiovascular magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is an emerging modality that offers unique measurements and observations to cardiologists. Although echocardiography, X-ray angiography, and multi-detector CT modalities are conventionally used in clinical practice, cardiac MRI is a noninvasive and complementary technique that offers gold standard measurements for the assessment of regional and global cardiac function, myocardial infarction, and coronary artery disease.

    However, the acquisition of high spatial resolution cardiac images is challenging due to respiratory-induced heart motion. Prospective compensation of the respiratory motion unpredictably prolongs scan-time that impedes the acquisition of images with a high spatial resolution. In this talk, the conventional prospective respiratory motion compensation algorithm in cardiac MRI is briefly presented. A new adaptive algorithm is then introduced that minimizes the respiratory-induced heart motion in a predictable scan-time. Next, a novel algorithm based on Compressed Sensing (CS) is presented to reduce scan-time while minimizing the respiratory motion of the heart. The reduction in scan-time can be used as a tradeoff to improve image spatial resolution.

  • Speaker Biography: Mehdi Hedjazi Moghari was born in Iran. He received B.Sc. and M.Sc. degrees in Electrical Engineering from Amirkabir University of Technology (Tehran Polytechnic), Tehran, Iran in 1999 and 2002. Mehdi received Ph.D. degree in Electrical and Computer Engineering at Queen's University, Kingston, Ontario, Canada, in 2008. During 2009 and 2012, he continued his research at the cardiac MRI Center of Harvard Medical School at Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center, Boston, MA, USA. He is now with the Cardiology Department of Boston Children's Hospital. His research interest is cardiovascular MRI including: pulse sequence design, motion compensation, image registration, and reconstruction.

Life at the Google
Cloud based machine learning, Chrome experimentation and life at the Google


Speaker: Robert Kaplow, Software Developer, Google

When: 4 PM - 5PM, October 29, 2012

Where: McConnell Engineering Building, room 603

Light refreshments will be served.

  • Abstract: In this talk, I will discuss the projects I have been involved in as a software developer at Google, specifically the Prediction API and Chrome web browser. The Prediction API is a cloud based machine learning service which can handle regression and classification tasks. The input data to this service is supplied by a user, and a RESTful API call triggers a machine learning system working in the back-end. The results can be received on-demand via another API call. In the first part of my talk, I will present the overall system and challenges in development of the Prediction API technology. In the second part, I will discuss my work in providing tools for A/B testing on new Chrome features. The Chrome web browser is one of the most popular browsers today and there has been increasing demand for large scale experimentation on this platform.
    Finally, I will also talk about working in software engineering industry, specifically how it is done at Google.

  • Speaker Biography: Robert Kaplow is a software developer at Google. He recently joined the Chrome team and has been working on improving tools for testing new experimental Chrome features. Previously, he worked in the Google Research group on the Prediction API and in machine learning infrastructure. Robert completed Bachelor's and Master's degree in Computer Science at McGill University. His research at McGill focused on probabilistic decision making and reinforcement learning.

Technical Talk
Pushing the Envelope: Oscillators beyond the Conventional Limits


Speaker:Ehsan Afshari, Associate Professor, Cornell University

When:11 AM-12:15PM, June 18, 2012

Where: McConnell Engineering Building, room 603

  • Abstract: There is a growing interest in terahertz systems for compact, low cost and energy efficient imaging, spectroscopy, and high data rate communication. In this talk, after a brief introduction to our research at Cornell University, we present a novel high power varactor-less VCO at sub-mm-wave and terahertz frequencies. This circuit works based on the theory of coupled oscillators in a ring structure and efficiently generates and combines harmonics from multiple core oscillators. Moreover by adjusting the coupling between oscillators, the frequency can be tuned. Using a standard 65 nm digital CMOS process, we show a 300 GHz VCO with ~7% tuning range and 0.76 mW of output power. The generated power is higher than any other CMOS or compound semiconductor source and at the same time shows the highest tuning range among terahertz CMOS sources. We will also show a 2-D phased array with more than 1 mW radiated power at 350GHz and another broadband signal source at 480GHz with more than 0.2mW generated power over a 40GHz bandwidth.

    Next, we show a novel dual-band oscillator with low phase noise performance. This idea leads to a VCO with more than 100% tuning range (2.48 – 5.8 GHz) and extremely low phase noise that satisfies the phase noise requirement for all cellular bands while only taking the area of a fixed-frequency oscillator. Finally, we introduce a resonant parametric amplifier/oscillator with an enhanced noise performance by exploiting the noise-squeezing effect. Noise squeezing occurs through the phase-sensitive amplification process and suppresses one of the two quadrature components of the input noise. This structure leads to a noise figure around 0dB for a narrowband signal at around 10 GHz in standard 130 nm CMOS process. It can also be exploited to reduce the phase noise of an oscillator by increasing its amplitude noise.
    Biography: Ehsan Afshari was born in 1979. He received the B.Sc. degree in Electronics Engineering from the Sharif University of Technology, Tehran, Iran and the M.S. and Ph.D. degree in electrical engineering from the California Institute of Technology, Pasadena, in 2003, and 2006, respectively. In August 2006, he joined the faculty in Electrical and Computer Engineering at Cornell University. His research interests are mm-wave and terahertz electronics and low-noise integrated circuits for applications in communication systems, sensing, and biomedical devices.

    Prof. Afshari serves as the chair of the IEEE Ithaca section, as the chair of Cornell Highly Integrated Physical Systems (CHIPS), as a member of International Technical Committee of the IEEE Solid-State Circuit Conference (ISSCC), as a member of the Analog Signal Processing Technical Committee of the IEEE Circuits and Systems Society, as a member of the Technical Program Committee of the IEEE Custom Integrated Circuits Conference (CICC), and as a member of Technical Program Committee of the IEEE International Conference on Ultra-Wideband (ICUWB)
